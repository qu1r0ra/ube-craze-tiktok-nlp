# More Than Purple: Ube The Filipino Cultural Icon

1. **Background of the Study**
   What happens when a food deeply rooted in one culture becomes a global trend? The international popularity of ube has introduced Filipino cuisine to new audiences through social media, food blogs, and digital marketing. Once primarily used with Filipino desserts and celebrations, ube is now featured in cafes, bakeries, and food products around the world, showing how digital media can transform a culturally significant food into a global phenomenon (The Economist, 2026).
   Ube (_Dioscorea alata_), otherwise known as purple yam, has long been a staple ingredient in Filipino cuisine. It is used in traditional delicacies such as ube halaya, halo-halo, sapin-sapin, and various pastries and desserts. Ube carries cultural significance for a lot of Filipinos because it is often associated with family gatherings, celebrations, and shared traditions. As Filipino migration and globalization have increased the visibility of Filipino food abroad, ube has emerged as one of the most recognizable symbols of Filipino cuisine.
   The global popularity of ube has been further accelerated by the rise of digital media. Social media platforms such as TikTok, Instagram, X (previously Twitter), and YouTube have allowed food trends to broaden their reach across the globe, allowing local dishes and ingredients to be exposed to international audiences. According to Zhang (2021), ube’s distinctive appearance, versatility, and cultural significance have contributed to its rising popularity among international consumers, transforming it from a familiar Filipino ingredient into a globally recognized food trend. Its rising fame can be understood through the concept of gastronationalism, which refers to the relationship between food and national identity (DeSoucey, 2010). Food often serves as a cultural symbol by which communities express, protect, and promote their heritage.
   The global rise of ube illustrates this phenomenon. While digital media has boosted the visibility of Filipino cuisine and presented it to international audiences, it has also sparked arguments on cultural ownership, authenticity, and representation. As traditional foods become global commodities, questions arise about who gets to define their meaning and how their cultural origins are acknowledged. Examining ube through the lens of digital gastronationalism, therefore, offers insights into how food, identity, and culture are negotiated in an increasingly interconnected digital world.

2. **Significance of the Topic**
   In today’s world, digital media has become the central arena where cultural identities are negotiated, celebrated, and contested (Yao 2025; Zhang and Shen 2025). For Filipinos and the diaspora, ube is far more than a fleeting food trend; it is a deeply sentimental emblem of home, comfort, and collective memory. From the _binignit_, _halaya_, and _sapin-sapin_ shared at family gatherings, _ube_ carries with it the nostalgia of childhood, the warmth of community, and the continuity of cultural heritage (De Villa et al. 2022; Miano et al. 2025). Its presence in Filipino cuisine is more than just culinary. It is emotional, symbolic, and profoundly tied to identity.
   This research project is important as it highlights the dual role of digital platforms. Specifically, while they _can_ amplify cultural pride, they can also dilute it. When global trends celebrate _ube_ only for its striking purple hue or market it as an exotic novelty without acknowledging its history, the ingredient risks being stripped of its cultural meaning. For the communities that nurtured it, this can feel like a form of erasure, a concern also observed in research on Māori _hāngī_, where scholars such as Richardson (2024) document anxieties about diminished cultural ownership under commercialization. Conversely, gastronationalism empowers Filipinos to reclaim their narrative, assert agency, and use online spaces as instruments of soft power, echoing studies showing how Filipino youth and migrants strategically shape identity and public discourse through social media (Agojo et al. 2023; Umel 2022). By studying this phenomenon, we gain insight into how modern communities defend cultural boundaries while navigating globalization.
   The relevance of this topic speaks to how cultures are represented, commodified, and contested in the digital age. Raising awareness about digital gastronationalism fosters a more mindful approach to global consumption by revealing how online food narratives shape cultural meaning (Jin 2025). Research on media and food literacy shows that such awareness shifts audiences from passive consumption toward more critical and culturally informed engagement (Chen et al. 2020; Truman and Elliott 2019), while studies of social‑media food discourse further demonstrate that understanding the construction of food narratives supports deeper respect for cultural context and heritage (Steils and Obaidalahe 2020). For both the academic community and the general public, this project offers a timely example of how national pride operates in digital spaces. By centering _ube_ within the framework of digital gastronationalism, this advocacy campaign seeks to remind audiences that what may look like a simple dessert carries generations of memory, identity, and pride. Our goal is to inspire a deeper respect for Filipino cuisine so that when the world enjoys _ube_, it is not only for its color or novelty, but with an understanding of the culture and people who have cherished it for centuries.

3. **Scope and Limitations**
   As student researchers who grew up in a hyper-connected world due to the proliferation of the Internet and social media, we notice that our experiences and beliefs significantly shape our perceptions of cultural trends online. Thus, we focus our study on digital gastronationalism, exploring how Filipinos leverage social media platforms to assert cultural ownership. Our research further focuses on the global commercialization of ube, analyzing Filipino reactions to foreign content creators and corporate entities that utilize or commodify the ingredient.
   To achieve this, we will analyze data solely from TikTok, gathering public video content, captions, and comment metadata published between 2024 and 2026\. To ensure the project remains achievable within a six-week timeline, we chose to scope down our data collection to TikTok. Thus, we will not be gathering content from other social media platforms. To accomplish this, we will use a Python script to extract metadata from the top 30 highest-engagement ube-related videos on TikTok based on platform search metrics. For each video, we will use the top 100 most-liked comments, including their direct reply threads. Finally, we will filter out foreign-language commentary to only include English, Tagalog, and Taglish text strings.

   Using the collected data, we will employ Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques (e.g., tokenization, text normalization, keyword frequency distribution analysis) to map and analyze sentiment patterns. We intend to use our insights from these to complement our sociological and historical analysis of Filipino digital gastronationalism. The primary target audience for both our research paper and the accompanying short documentary consists of Gen Z digital natives, Filipino university students, and global online consumers of food trends.

   This study will only focus on ube and will not discuss other Filipino foods that may also represent Filipino gastronationalism. The research will be limited to selected online content and will not include interviews with content creators, consumers, or people in the food industry. Furthermore, the study will not examine the economic effects of the ube trend or how it directly affects people’s views about Filipino identity. Since the findings are limited to a small amount of digital content, the findings may not fully represent how ube is discussed across the Internet.

4. **Medium to be Used**
   The most effective way to present the topic is through a short documentary video. It uses elements such as visual, textual, and auditory to successfully deliver the message. The rising popularity of the ube craze was mainly rooted in the large influence of social media; video content was widely used to present food trends, advertisements, and digital storytelling. People learn effectively through words and visuals rather than text alone. This makes a short documentary video suitable for presenting the historical and cultural significance of ube while also incorporating the contribution of social media to its rising popularity. Moreover, the use of documentaries is also effective in presenting culture as it uses factual information in a form of storytelling, which is engaging for viewers to help better understand the topic.
   The short documentary video style will engage and inform the intended audience through visual storytelling, presenting social media video examples, narration, and visual images. By presenting images of traditional ube delicacies or products together with the rising popularity of ube in the contemporary world that are featured on digital platforms, the audience will be able to identify how food can be connected to cultural representation and national identity.
